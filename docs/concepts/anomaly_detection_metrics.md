# Anomaly Detection Metrics: The Theory Behind AUPIMO, AUROC, and AUPRO

**Based on:** *AUPIMO: Redefining Anomaly Localization Benchmarks with High Speed and Low Tolerance*
**Authors:** Joao P. C. Bertoldo, Dick Ameln, Ashwin Vaidya, Samet Akçay

**Key Concept:** Anomaly localization metrics like AUROC and AUPRO are inflating model performance, making public benchmarks (like MVTec AD and VisA) appear "solved." The authors introduce AUPIMO (Area Under the Per-IMage Overlap), a novel, fast, and strict metric that redefines how visual anomaly detection (AD) models are evaluated.

---

## 1. The Core Problem in Anomaly Detection (AD)

In Visual Anomaly Detection, models are trained exclusively on "normal" images. The goal is to detect any deviation (anomalies) at inference time. Because the anomalous class is unknown and unbounded, it has an unlimited number of modes.

The paper argues that existing evaluation metrics are fundamentally flawed for two reasons:

1. **Extreme Class Imbalance:** Normal pixels vastly outnumber anomalous pixels, artificially inflating traditional pixel-level classification metrics (like Accuracy).
2. **Bias Towards Known Anomalies:** Validating a model (e.g., picking binarization thresholds) based on anomalous images conditions the model on the known anomalies in the test set, violating the unsupervised nature of the AD task.

---

## 2. Mathematical Precursors: AUROC and AUPRO

To understand AUPIMO, we first look at the mathematical definitions of its predecessors.

**Notation:**

*   $a \in \mathbb{R}_+^M$: Anomaly score map for an image with $M$ pixels.
*   $y \in \{0, 1\}^M$: Ground truth mask.
*   $r$: A region mask (a maximally connected anomalous component).
*   $t$: Binarization threshold.

Performance is measured at different scopes:

*   **Set Scope ($s$):** Aggregates all pixels across all images.
*   **Image Scope ($i$):** Aggregates pixels within a single image.
*   **Region Scope ($r$):** Aggregates pixels within a specific anomalous region $r$.

### AUROC (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic)

AUROC evaluates anomaly localization as a pure pixel-level binary classification task.

*   **Set FPR (False Positive Rate):**
    $$F_s(t) = \frac{\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} |(a \ge t) \wedge (\neg y)|}{\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} |\neg y|}$$
*   **Set TPR (True Positive Rate / [Recall](#appendix-data-science-basics)):**
    $$T_s(t) = \frac{\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} |(a \ge t) \wedge y|}{\sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} |y|}$$
*   **AUROC Integral:**
    $$\int_0^1 T_s(F_s^{-1}(z)) \, dz$$

**Flaw:** Confounding all pixels ignores the structural properties of individual images and is heavily skewed by the massive amount of normal pixels.

### AUPRO (Area Under the Per-Region Overlap)

AUPRO was designed to fix AUROC's bias toward large anomalies by giving equal weight to every connected anomalous region, regardless of its size.

*   **Region TPR:**
    $$T_r(t) = \frac{|(a \ge t) \wedge r|}{|r|}$$
*   **Average Region TPR:**
    $$\overline{T_r}(t) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{R}|} \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} T_r^r(t)$$
    *(Average [recall](#appendix-data-science-basics) across all regions $\mathcal{R}$ in the dataset).*
*   **AUPRO Integral:**
    $$\frac{1}{U} \int_0^U \overline{T_r}(F_s^{-1}(z)) \, dz$$

**Flaw:** It integrates up to an upper bound $U$ (typically $U=0.3$, or 30% Set FPR). While this acts as a validation requirement, the Set FPR ($F_s$) includes normal pixels from anomalous images. Furthermore, calculating connected components makes AUPRO computationally expensive.

## 3. The New Standard: AUPIMO

AUPIMO introduces two radical shifts: **(1) Normal-only validation** and **(2) Per-image scoring.**

### Mathematical Definition of AUPIMO

Instead of calculating the False Positive Rate over all images, AUPIMO defines a Shared FPR ($F_{sh}$) strictly computed on normal images only ($\mathcal{Y}^0$). This removes the bias of knowing the anomalies.

*   **Shared FPR ($F_{sh}$):**
    $$F_{sh}(t) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Y}^0|} \sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}^0} F_i^y(t)$$
    *(Average of the image-scoped FPR across all normal images).*

*   **Image TPR ($T_i$):**
    $$T_i(t) = \frac{|(a \ge t) \wedge y|}{|y|}$$
    *(Recall calculated strictly within a single anomalous image).*

### The PIMO Curve

For every single anomalous image, a PIMO curve is generated by plotting its Image TPR against the logarithm of the Shared FPR:

$$PIMO: t \mapsto (\log(F_{sh}(t)), T_i(t))$$

### The AUPIMO Integral

The area under this per-image curve is calculated within a strictly bounded, log-scaled FPR range:

$$AUPIMO = \int_{\log(L)}^{\log(U)} \frac{T_i(F_{sh}^{-1}(z))}{\log(U/L)} \, d\log(z)$$

### The Integration Bounds: "Low Tolerance"

By default, AUPIMO uses extremely strict bounds: $L = 10^{-5}$ and $U = 10^{-4}$.

At these levels, false positive regions on normal images are barely visible (usually less than a few pixels).

An AUPIMO score represents: *"The segmentation recall on this anomalous image, given that the model yields practically zero false positives on normal images."*

---

## 4. Key Properties & Advantages

*   **Bias-Free Validation:** Because thresholds are indexed by an FPR calculated only on normal images, the metric is entirely blind to the types of anomalies present in the test set. It isolates the model's ability to handle normal variance from its ability to spot anomalies.
*   **High Speed:** Because AUPIMO relies on simple pixel-ratio metrics at the image scope (rather than extracting connected components like AUPRO), it is mathematically simpler and drastically faster to compute on both CPUs and GPUs.
*   **Statistical Robustness:** Generating an individual score for every image (rather than a single dataset-wide score) allows researchers to visualize score distributions (e.g., boxplots). It enables the use of statistical tools like the Wilcoxon signed-rank test for robust model ablation studies.
*   **Resilience to Noisy Annotations:** Some datasets (like VisA) have tiny, 1-pixel annotation errors. AUPRO gives these 1-pixel errors the exact same weight as a massive defect. Because AUPIMO uses image-scoped pixel ratios, these tiny annotation errors have almost zero mathematical impact on the final score.

## 5. Experimental Insights and "The Unsolved Benchmark"

The authors benchmarked 8 State-of-the-Art models (including PatchCore, EfficientAD, PaDiM, SimpleNet, and FastFlow) across 27 datasets (MVTec AD and VisA).

**Crucial Findings:**

*   **The Benchmarks Are Not Solved:** While AUROC and AUPRO scores routinely hover around 98%-99%, applying AUPIMO's strict low-FP tolerance causes performance to plummet. Models frequently fail to identify anomalies without triggering unacceptable amounts of false positives on normal data.
*   **High Variance:** Best-in-class models often have an average AUPIMO of 60%-70% but exhibit a massive distribution variance, effectively scoring 0% on harder image instances.
*   **The $P_{33}$ Metric:** The authors propose evaluating models based on the $33^{rd}$ percentile of AUPIMO scores ($P_{33}$) to measure worst-case, rigorous reliability.
*   **No Universal Winner:** PatchCore excels in many areas but completely fails on datasets like VisA's "Macaroni 2". Conversely, EfficientAD handles Macaroni 2 quite well. The authors conclude that problem-specific model choice is highly advisable—there is currently no single model architecture that universally generalizes across all anomaly types under strict tolerances.

---

## 6. Limitations and Flaws of AUPIMO

The authors outline a few specific limitations of the AUPIMO metric in their conclusion and appendices:

*   **It Measures Recall, Not Precision:** AUPIMO is strictly a recall metric. It evaluates how much of the ground-truth anomaly was found, but it completely ignores the "segmentation quality" or [precision](#appendix-data-science-basics) within an anomalous image. If a model correctly identifies a tiny scratch but vastly over-predicts its size (coloring half the image as an anomaly), AUPIMO does not penalize this—as long as the model maintains low false positives on normal images. To address this, the authors suggest exploring an Intersection-over-Union (IoU) variant based on the same framework.
*   **The "Multiple Anomalies" Masking Effect:** Because AUPIMO calculates recall at the image scope (rather than the region scope like AUPRO), it struggles when multiple distinct anomalies are present in a single image. If an image contains two separate defects and the model perfectly detects one but completely misses the other, AUPIMO will return a middling score (e.g., ~50%). This averages out a total success and a total failure, hiding the fact that an entire anomaly was missed.
*   **Resolution Dependence:** The default integration bounds of $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$ FPR are extremely strict and assume a reasonably high-resolution image. For instance, in a $256 \times 256$ image, a single pixel represents a relative area of $\approx 1.5 \times 10^{-5}$. If a researcher applies AUPIMO to very low-resolution images (e.g., $64 \times 64$), the default bounds become mathematically meaningless because they represent a fraction of a single pixel.

## Appendix: Data Science Basics

To fully grasp the mechanics of metrics like AUPIMO and AUROC, it is essential to understand the foundational concepts of binary classification. In Anomaly Detection, we evaluate every single pixel.

### The Confusion Matrix

When a model predicts whether a pixel is normal or anomalous, there are four possible outcomes:

*   **True Positive (TP):** The pixel is an anomaly, and the model correctly predicted it as an anomaly.
*   **True Negative (TN):** The pixel is normal, and the model correctly predicted it as normal.
*   **False Positive (FP):** The pixel is normal, but the model incorrectly predicted it as an anomaly *(Type I Error / False Alarm)*.
*   **False Negative (FN):** The pixel is an anomaly, but the model incorrectly predicted it as normal *(Type II Error / Missed Defect)*.

### Foundational Metrics

Based on these four outcomes, we can calculate several key ratios:

#### 1. Recall (True Positive Rate / TPR)
Recall measures the proportion of actual anomalies that were correctly identified. In the context of segmentation, it tells us "how much of the defect area was found."

$$Recall = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}$$

*AUPIMO focuses heavily on Recall at the image scope ($T_i$).*

#### 2. Precision
Precision measures the proportion of predicted anomalies that were actually real anomalies. It tells us "when the model flags a defect, how often is it correct?"

$$Precision = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$$

*If a model flags the entire image as an anomaly, its Recall is 100%, but its Precision is extremely low. AUPIMO does not currently measure precision inside an anomalous image.*

#### 3. False Positive Rate (FPR)
FPR measures the proportion of actual normal pixels that were incorrectly flagged as anomalies.

$$FPR = \frac{FP}{FP + TN}$$

*In Anomaly Detection, maintaining a practically zero FPR is crucial because in a real-world scenario, you process thousands of normal images. Even a 1% FPR would result in a massive number of false alarms. This is why AUPIMO bounds FPR between $10^{-5}$ and $10^{-4}$.*

#### 4. Accuracy
Accuracy is the proportion of total correct predictions (both normal and anomalous) out of all predictions.

$$Accuracy = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}$$

***Why Accuracy Fails in AD:** In an image, 99.9% of pixels might be normal. If a model simply predicts "Normal" for every single pixel, its Accuracy will be 99.9%, even though it completely failed to detect the anomaly. This is known as the **Class Imbalance Problem**.*
